The Problem

It's no secret that government agency websites rarely get the attnetion they need and/or deserve. The current structure of the Department of the Interior website yields a user experience that is often times counter-intuitive, navigation filled with repetition, and lacks overall visual appeal.

The Solution

Through testing and analysis of the current site, execute a re-design of the Department of the Interior website which offers a more user friendly experience and is more visually appealing.

Specs of the Project

  • •Timeline: 2.5 weeks, independent work
  • •Tools: Canva, Invision, Miro, Figma
  • •Re-Design based on defined user path

You gotta start at the start...

-User Research and Analysis

User testing of current Deprtment of the Interior Website

  • conduct 5 user interviews gathering preliminary info
  • conduct 5 user tests of current site
  • synthesize feedback into user insights

    • So, who is our user?

    -User Insights, Proto Persona

    Organizing the data, Analysis of of insights, defining the user

  • Information Seekers - user who needs information/resources on the DOI
  • Casual Browser - user who is curious about the DOI
  • Mission Specific - user who comes to the page for a specific reason only
  • Students - user who is directed to the page for educational purposes

    • In my user testing, I defined a user path to find volunteer opportunities within the DOI website. To further develop my user and clearly outline the direction of the project, I created a persona that would fit into the 'mission specific' category. Let's meet her...

    ...And away we go!

    From here it was time to dive headfirst into the project.

    Making Sense of the Architecture

    I knew I wanted to build out the volunteer opportunity pathway but it was crticial to streamline the information architecture to make the navigation more clear. Time for a card sort (virtually of course, you know...Covid)!

    Simplified Navigation

    With so much content, the nagivation needed to be simplified. Too much repetition between the header and the footer made everything too clunky. I streamlined the presentation making it more user friendly.

    Mapping Out the Journey

    If I was going to refresh the user experience, I needed a better understanding of what options the current version presented. I outlined a wireflow of the current volunteer pathway.

    Wireframing

    With research in hand, it was time to lay out what the new version of the site was going to look like. Using Figma, I was able to put together wireframes of the new flow. Through iteration, I was able to simplify the pathway, expand on information, and keep the user on the DOI main site with only one external link.

    Style, Style, Style

    “Things aren’t always #000000 AND #FFFFFF”

    Visual design was an important part of this project. How was I going to bring a fresh pespective, visually, without overloading the user or packing too much on the pages so that the information is now obscured by imagery? I called upon elements from Native American culture, National Park photography, and a color pallete that was inspired by nature.

    Hi-Fidelity Prototypes

    With wireframes and style guide completed, it was time to make the project come to life. I carefully constructed both a desktop and mobile prototype which tied together nearly 3 weeks of research and design.

    Learning, Listening, Applying

    I believe this quote from Dain Miller sums up this project best - “UI is the saddle, the stirrups, & the reins. UX is the feeling you get being able to ride the horse.”

    In order to execute a successful re-design, you have to consider the following:

  • Who are my users and what are their needs?
  • Where does the current version fail and how do we improve it?
  • With great vision comes great responsibility.

    • In a very short period of time I was able to research, define, iterate, and design a new interpretation of an existing government agency website. Through application of all of these processes I was able to present a final product that, I believe, improves upon the existing version while encorporating my personal aethestic. Ultimately the biggest takeaway from this work is that no good design can be brought to life unless you take the journey in your user’s shoes.
